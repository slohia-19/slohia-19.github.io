BidReady Berkeley

Designing an accessible on-ramp for small businesses to participate in City of Berkeley municipal contracting.

Timeline: 1 Week Role: Product Manager City of Berkeley Civic Innovation Challenge
The Problem

Small and medium-sized businesses are underrepresented in city contracting due to barriers around awareness, resources, and process complexity.

My Role

PM lead applying a product management framework — problem framing, user research synthesis, solution scoping, and roadmap prioritization.

The Team

Interdisciplinary team of MBA, MPP, and data science students collaborating for the first time under a one-week sprint.

Outcome

Delivered a full product strategy — user persona, MVP feature set, and phased roadmap — to the City of Berkeley sponsor.

Background & Context

Small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) are underrepresented in city contracting — not necessarily due to lack of interest, but due to systemic barriers around awareness, resources, and process complexity. The City of Berkeley wanted to diversify the pool of businesses bidding on and receiving municipal contracts.

Over one week, I worked on an interdisciplinary team — MBA, MPP, and data science — people who had never worked together before — to design a solution using a product management framework.

Defining the Problem

The first step was defining who we were solving for and why the problem existed. Our primary user segment was small and medium business owners. Getting crisp on the problem statement early was essential — without a focused scope, we risked building a solution to the wrong problem.

Key questions we asked early on:

  • Why aren't SMBs bidding on city contracts today?
  • Is the barrier a lack of awareness, a lack of resources, or a confusing process?
  • What would "good" look like from both the city's and the business owner's perspective?
Problem Statement: Small and medium business owners need a simpler, more accessible way to discover and apply for city contracts because the current process is too complex, time-consuming, and hard to navigate without dedicated resources.

Exploring the Solution Space

Before narrowing in, we mapped out several directions the team could pursue:

A

Digital platform — streamline applications and provide education for small businesses.

B

Policy changes — establish a dedicated task force or increase city staffing.

C

Community council — leverage local business leaders and organizations to spread awareness.

We needed user research to decide which direction had the most leverage.

User Research

We conducted research across two groups to ground our decisions in evidence rather than assumptions.

Stakeholder Interviews

We spoke directly with our city sponsor to understand existing processes, current pain points from the city's perspective, and available data on contract participation.

Business Owner Interviews

We conducted interviews with local business owners and business associations to ground our understanding in lived experience and narrow our scope to what actually mattered to users.

Key Insights

Synthesizing our qualitative research, four themes emerged:

01

The process is too long and confusing — the language and length of current forms creates significant friction.

02

SMBs have limited capacity — small business owners wear many hats and don't have time to navigate complex procurement processes.

03

Social media is underutilized — there's an opportunity to reach businesses where they already are.

04

Trust matters — businesses respond to outreach from trusted messengers and community partners, not just city communications.

The Solution

Based on these insights, we focused on building a more accessible on-ramp — improving awareness, onboarding, and communication pathways before optimizing the application process itself. The MVP question was simple: how do we get people in the door first?

1. Community Advisory Task Force

A representative council modeled after UC Berkeley's Procurement meetings, including voices from multiple community groups. Goals:

  • Map the current bidder journey
  • Facilitate listening sessions with underrepresented business communities
  • Champion opportunities through trusted community networks
2. Technology Solution

A guided onboarding experience and process checklist, paired with an improved opportunity dashboard with filtering capabilities.

MVP Features
  • Step-by-step onboarding flow for first-time bidders
  • Filterable dashboard to browse open opportunities
Future Roadmap
  • Streamlined and simplified application forms
  • Social media outreach integration
  • Live help and chat assistance
  • Joint bid matchmaking system

Success Metrics

We defined four metrics to track the effectiveness of the solution across process, engagement, reach, and equity dimensions.

Metric
What It Measures
Time to complete application
Reduction in process friction
Opportunity dashboard engagement
Improved discoverability
Social media reach
Effectiveness of outreach channels
% of bids from women- & minority-owned SMBs
Equity impact over time

Learnings & Takeaways

  • A strong problem statement is everything. We can't solve every dimension of procurement inequity in one week. Narrowing scope early allowed the team to go deep on what actually mattered instead of spreading thin across too many directions.
  • Wear multiple hats. Working across MBA, MPP, and data science disciplines pushed me to think simultaneously like a designer, a policymaker, and a business owner — each lens revealed something the others missed.
  • User insight drives better solutions. The pivot toward on-ramp accessibility (vs. jumping straight to form optimization) came directly from interviews. Without that research, we likely would have built the wrong thing.
  • MVP thinking unlocks momentum. Rather than waiting for a perfect solution, we segmented the roadmap into release slices — prioritizing shipping meaningful improvements early and iterating from there.
